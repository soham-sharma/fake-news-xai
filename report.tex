% ── Abstract ────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\begin{abstract}
Automated fake news detection systems are deployed at scale, yet the
internal logic of high-performing models is rarely examined.  This paper
compares two fundamentally different classification architectures on the
ISOT Fake News Dataset: a Decision Tree operating on TF-IDF features
(Approach A, white-box) and a fine-tuned DistilRoBERTa transformer
(Approach B, black-box).  SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) are
applied to both.  For the Decision Tree, SHAP values are computed
exactly via \texttt{shap.TreeExplainer}; for DistilRoBERTa, a
partition-based approximation over token coalitions is used.  We
evaluate explanations across three dimensions: classification
performance (H1), explanation stability measured over 50 near-duplicate
article pairs (H2), and explanation fidelity measured via sufficiency
and necessity masking at $k \in \{3,5,10\}$ (H3-fidelity).  A
critical finding is that the ISOT dataset contains severe lexical
leakage: the Reuters news agency dateline pattern \texttt{(Reuters)}
appears in 21{,}256 articles with a 99.96\% label-skew toward the real
class.  We introduce a lexical debiasing pass that strips 12 news
agency patterns before training, producing parallel original and
debiased experimental splits.  On the original split, the Decision Tree
achieves F1\,=\,0.9963 and DistilRoBERTa achieves F1\,=\,0.9997.
After debiasing, the Decision Tree drops to F1\,=\,0.9649 while
DistilRoBERTa is essentially unchanged at F1\,=\,0.9996, confirming
that the Decision Tree relied heavily on lexical shortcuts.
Explanation stability (mean Pearson $r$) is significantly higher for
the Decision Tree on the original split ($r=0.9999$, $p < 10^{-9}$)
but the advantage disappears after debiasing ($p=0.101$), indicating
the advantage was leakage-driven.  Decision Tree SHAP consistently
achieves higher composite fidelity on both splits, a result that is
partly structural: exact SHAP and decision tree predictions are two
descriptions of the same computation.
\end{abstract}

% ── I. Introduction ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Misinformation circulates on social media at a scale and speed that
precludes manual review.  Studies have linked the spread of false
content to measurable effects on electoral behavior~\cite{vosoughi2018spread},
vaccine hesitancy~\cite{roozenbeek2020susceptibility}, and public health
responses during disease outbreaks.  Automated classifiers trained on
labeled datasets can flag content at inference time, but they are
typically deployed as black boxes.  For content moderation, this creates
two problems.  First, when a model makes an incorrect decision,
moderators cannot audit the reasoning behind it.  Second, in several
jurisdictions, automated decision systems that affect users carry
disclosure requirements that black-box models cannot satisfy.

Interpretable fake news detection is therefore not merely an academic
target.  There is a practical need for models whose decision logic is
auditable by non-technical reviewers, and whose explanations are
reliable enough to be acted on.

This paper addresses that need through a systematic comparison of a
Decision Tree and a fine-tuned DistilRoBERTa transformer, both trained
on the ISOT Fake News Dataset~\cite{ahmed2018detecting}.  SHAP is applied
to both.  The comparison goes beyond classification metrics to examine
the explanations themselves across three dimensions:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Performance (H1):} Does DistilRoBERTa achieve higher
        classification accuracy than the Decision Tree?
  \item \textbf{Stability (H2):} Are Decision Tree SHAP explanations
        more consistent across near-duplicate inputs than post-hoc
        BERT SHAP explanations?
  \item \textbf{Fidelity:} Do the SHAP explanations faithfully reflect
        each model's actual decision logic, as measured by sufficiency
        and necessity masking?
\end{itemize}

A key contribution is the identification and systematic treatment of
lexical leakage in the ISOT dataset.  We show that the Reuters news
agency byline, \texttt{(Reuters)}, is present in over 21{,}000 articles
with a label skew exceeding 99.9 percentage points.  A model trained on
this data learns source attribution, not misinformation detection.  We
introduce a lexical debiasing step that removes 12 identified news
agency patterns, produce parallel experimental splits, and train both
models independently on each split.  All reported results compare
original and debiased conditions.

% ── II. Related Work ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Fake News Detection}
The dominant approach to automated fake news detection has evolved from
early bag-of-words classifiers~\cite{perez2018automatic} through
shallow machine learning pipelines using TF-IDF representations and
ensemble methods, to deep learning approaches using recurrent
networks~\cite{ruchansky2017csi} and, more recently, pre-trained
transformers.  Zhou and Zafarani~\cite{zhou2020survey} provide a
comprehensive survey and note that reported accuracy figures are highly
dataset-dependent, with results on biased benchmarks often
overestimating real-world performance.  The ISOT dataset~\cite{ahmed2018detecting}
is widely used but, as we demonstrate, contains a structural source
attribution shortcut that inflates performance on all classifiers.

\subsection{Explainability in NLP Classification}
SHAP~\cite{lundberg2017unified} provides a game-theoretic framework for
feature attribution grounded in Shapley values from cooperative game
theory.  For tree-based models, \texttt{TreeExplainer}~\cite{lundberg2020local}
computes exact Shapley values in polynomial time.  For neural models,
approximation is required.  The \texttt{shap.Explainer} with a
\texttt{Text} masker uses a partition-based algorithm that samples token
coalitions and estimates the marginal contribution of each token by
measuring how the model output changes when it is masked.

LIME~\cite{ribeiro2016should} provides an alternative post-hoc
explanation approach via local surrogate models.  Both SHAP and LIME
have been criticized for instability: small input perturbations can
produce large changes in attributions~\cite{alvarez2018robustness}.
Yeh et al.~\cite{yeh2019fidelity} propose fidelity metrics based on
sufficiency and necessity masking, which we adopt as a second evaluation
axis alongside stability.

\subsection{Dataset Bias and Leakage in NLP}
Gururangan et al.~\cite{gururangan2018annotation} demonstrate that NLI
datasets contain substantial annotation artifacts that allow shallow
classifiers to perform near human-level without genuine language
understanding.  Analogous source-attribution shortcuts have been
documented in news classification datasets.  Our work extends this line
of analysis to fake news detection, provides a systematic audit of
leakage patterns, and quantifies the effect of lexical debiasing on
both model accuracy and explanation quality.

% ── III. Dataset ─────────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Dataset}
\label{sec:dataset}

\subsection{ISOT Fake News Dataset}
The ISOT Fake News Dataset~\cite{ahmed2018detecting} contains 44{,}898
English-language news articles, with 21{,}417 labeled real (class 0)
and 23{,}481 labeled fake (class 1), yielding a 48\%/52\% class split.
The real articles are sourced from the Reuters news agency wire
service.  Fake articles were collected from sites flagged by PolitiFact
and other fact-checkers, including InfoWars and similar fringe outlets.
All articles include a title and body text, with an average combined
length of 419 words.

A stratified 80/20 split by label is applied using a fixed random seed
(42), yielding 35{,}918 training articles and 8{,}980 test articles.
The preprocessing pipeline lowercases all text, removes URLs and HTML
tags, and collapses whitespace.

\section{Methods}
\label{sec:methods}

\label{fig:system_architecture}
\end{figure*}

Fig.~\ref{fig:system_architecture} presents the end-to-end experimental
pipeline.  Both approaches are trained on the original and debiased
splits independently, producing four model--explainer configurations
whose outputs are compared along the three hypothesis dimensions
defined in Section~\ref{sec:intro}.

\subsection{Approach A: Decision Tree with TF-IDF Features}
\label{sec:approach_a}

\subsubsection{Feature Engineering}
Text features are extracted using TF-IDF vectorization over unigrams
and bigrams with a vocabulary ceiling of 5{,}000 terms and sublinear TF
scaling:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{tf\text{-}idf}(t,d)
    = \mathrm{tf}(t,d) \cdot \log\!\left(\frac{N}{\mathrm{df}(t)}\right)
  \label{eq:tfidf}
\end{equation}
where $N$ is the corpus size and $\mathrm{df}(t)$ is the number of
documents containing term $t$.  Eight additional metadata features are
appended: TextBlob sentiment polarity, TextBlob sentiment subjectivity,
Flesch-Kincaid grade level (via \texttt{textstat}), word count,
has-author binary flag, exclamation count, question mark count, and
uppercase letter ratio.  The combined feature matrix has 5{,}008
dimensions, stored as a sparse CSR matrix.

\subsubsection{Model and Hyperparameter Search}
A \texttt{DecisionTreeClassifier} with \texttt{class\_weight='balanced'}
is trained across a depth grid $d \in \{4, 6, 8, 10, 12\}$ and split
criteria \{gini, entropy\}, for ten configurations per split.
Selection uses 5-fold stratified cross-validation scored by mean F1.
An interpretability score is assigned to each configuration:
\begin{equation}
  I(T) = \frac{1}{\ln(L + 1)}
  \label{eq:interp}
\end{equation}
where $L$ is the number of leaves in tree $T$.  As $L$ grows with
depth, $I(T)$ decreases monotonically, providing a quantitative
representation of the readability cost of increased complexity.

\subsubsection{SHAP Computation}
Exact Shapley values are computed using \texttt{shap.TreeExplainer}:
\begin{equation}
  \phi_i(f) = \sum_{S \subseteq F \setminus \{i\}}
    \frac{|S|!\,(|F|-|S|-1)!}{|F|!}
    \bigl[f(S \cup \{i\}) - f(S)\bigr]
  \label{eq:shapley}
\end{equation}
where $F$ is the full feature set and $f(S)$ is the model output when
only features in $S$ are active.  For a decision tree, this can be
computed exactly in polynomial time by path enumeration, avoiding the
sampling approximation required by neural approaches.  Global importance
is computed as mean $|\phi_i|$ over all test samples.

\subsection{Approach B: DistilRoBERTa}
\label{sec:approach_b}

\subsubsection{Architecture and Training}
\texttt{distilroberta-base} is loaded from HuggingFace with an added
two-class sequence classification head.  Articles are tokenized with
the model's byte-pair encoding tokenizer, truncated to 256 tokens.
Fine-tuning uses the HuggingFace \texttt{Trainer} API with the
following hyperparameters: 3 epochs, learning rate $2 \times 10^{-5}$,
per-device batch size 32, mixed-precision training (fp16), best
checkpoint selection by validation F1.  A 10\% stratified validation
hold-out from the training split is used for checkpoint selection.
Training losses converge from 0.015 (epoch 1) to 0.002 (epoch 2) to
0.0002 (epoch 3).

\subsubsection{SHAP Computation}
Post-hoc SHAP explanations are computed using \texttt{shap.Explainer}
with a \texttt{shap.maskers.Text} masker, which replaces selected tokens
with the tokenizer's mask token and evaluates the model on different
coalitions.  The partition-based algorithm estimates~\eqref{eq:shapley}
by sampling rather than exact enumeration.  Token-level SHAP values are
aggregated to word level by summing contributions of subword pieces that
constitute the same word.  Global importance is computed as mean
$|\phi_i|$ over 500 randomly sampled test articles (runtime constraint:
approximately 14 hours on a single GPU for 500 examples).

\subsection{Evaluation Framework}
\label{sec:eval}

\subsubsection{Classification Metrics}
Standard binary classification metrics are reported: accuracy,
precision, recall, F1 (macro binary), and AUC-ROC.  All metrics are
computed on the held-out test split (8{,}980 articles).

\subsubsection{Explanation Stability}
Stability measures whether an explanation is consistent across
semantically similar inputs.  We generate 50 near-duplicate article
pairs from the test set using three perturbation strategies: (i)
synonym substitution via WordNet~\cite{miller1995wordnet}, replacing
2--3 content words per article; (ii) sentence reordering of all
non-first sentences; and (iii) minor lexical substitution (e.g.,
\emph{says} $\to$ \emph{stated}).  Only pairs with TF-IDF cosine
similarity $\geq 0.70$ between original and variant are retained.

For each pair $(x_1, x_2)$, SHAP importance vectors $\phi(x_1)$ and
$\phi(x_2)$ are computed and their Pearson correlation is measured:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Stab}(f, x_1, x_2)
    = r\!\left(\phi(x_1),\, \phi(x_2)\right)
  \label{eq:stability}
\end{equation}
The H2 test is a paired two-tailed $t$-test comparing the 50
per-pair stability scores from the Decision Tree against the 50
scores from DistilRoBERTa, on the same pairs.

\subsubsection{Explanation Fidelity}
Following Yeh et al.~\cite{yeh2019fidelity}, we measure fidelity through
sufficiency and necessity masking at $k \in \{3, 5, 10\}$.

\textbf{Sufficiency} ($\mathrm{Suff}_k$): the top-$k$ SHAP features
are retained; all others are zeroed (tabular) or replaced with the PAD
token (text).  Sufficiency is the fraction of predictions unchanged:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Suff}_k
    = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
      \mathbf{1}\!\left[\hat{f}\!\left(\mathrm{keep}_k(x_i)\right)
        = \hat{f}(x_i)\right]
  \label{eq:sufficiency}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Necessity} ($\mathrm{Nec}_k$): the top-$k$ SHAP features are
removed; all others are retained.  Necessity is the fraction of
predictions that change:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{Nec}_k
    = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
      \mathbf{1}\!\left[\hat{f}\!\left(\mathrm{drop}_k(x_i)\right)
        \neq \hat{f}(x_i)\right]
  \label{eq:necessity}
\end{equation}

\textbf{Composite fidelity} combines both:
\begin{equation}
  F_k = \frac{\mathrm{Suff}_k + \mathrm{Nec}_k}{2}
  \label{eq:fidelity}
\end{equation}

A random baseline is computed by repeating the sufficiency and
necessity tests with $k$ features selected uniformly at random, averaged
over 10 trials.  A faithful explanation must substantially exceed this
baseline.

\subsubsection{Decision Rules}
The white-box nature of the Decision Tree allows direct rule extraction.
On the original split, the first split at every path root is:
\begin{displaymath}
  \mathtt{reuters} > 0.010261 \Rightarrow \text{class} = 0,\ p = 1.00
\end{displaymath}
The tree is organized entirely around this one check.  On the debiased
split, the top rule reads:
\begin{displaymath}
  \mathtt{said} \leq 0.0316,\;
  \mathtt{video} \leq 0.0151,\;
  \mathtt{via} \leq 0.0039,\;\ldots
\end{displaymath}
These rules correspond to a writing-style fingerprint: low reporting-verb
frequency, absence of video references, and limited social-sharing
patterns are characteristic of real news in the debiased split.  Such
rules are directly auditable by a content moderator without NLP expertise.

On the original split, the Decision Tree achieves near-perfect stability
($r = 0.9999$, $\mathrm{std} = 4.3 \times 10^{-5}$) while

However, this result requires qualification.  The Decision Tree's
near-perfect stability is mechanistically explained by the leakage: both
the original and variant article either contain or do not contain the
\texttt{(Reuters)} token.  If they do, both traverse the same top-level
branch and produce identical SHAP values.  If neither does, the model
falls back to secondary features that are only minimally altered by
synonym substitution.  In either case, the SHAP vectors are effectively
identical, producing $r \approx 1$.

stability drops to $r = 0.874$ ($\mathrm{std} = 0.320$), while
DistilRoBERTa's stability is $r = 0.779$ ($\mathrm{std} = 0.177$).  The
$t$-test gives $p = 0.101$.  H2 is rejected on the debiased split: there
is no statistically significant stability difference between approaches.

The practical interpretation is that DT explanations are \emph{genuinely}
more stable than BERT explanations by a moderate margin (0.087
difference in mean $r$) after debiasing, but this difference does not
reach statistical significance at the 0.05 level with 50 pairs.

Several findings emerge from Table~\ref{tab:fidelity}.

\paragraph{DT Sufficiency = 1.000 on Original Split}
The Decision Tree achieves perfect sufficiency at all $k$ values on the
original split.  This is expected from the mathematical structure: for a
decision tree with exact SHAP, the top-$k$ SHAP features by magnitude
are precisely the features used on the traversed path.  Masking all
other features and re-running the model on this path produces an
identical prediction.  This is not a claim about model quality; it is a
consequence of the TreeExplainer computation being exact.

\paragraph{BERT Necessity = 0.000}
DistilRoBERTa exhibits zero necessity at $k = 3$ and $k = 5$ on the
original split, meaning that removing the top SHAP tokens changes
essentially no predictions.  This is a serious fidelity failure: the
tokens the explanation identifies as most important are not actually
necessary for the model's decision.  This outcome reflects the
approximation quality of the partition-based SHAP sampler and the fact
that DistilRoBERTa can redistribute attention across many tokens.

\paragraph{Fidelity Gap Persists After Debiasing}
On the debiased split, DT sufficiency at $k=3$ drops to 0.926 (no
longer guaranteed by exact SHAP alone), and necessity rises to 0.195.
BERT sufficiency at $k=3$ is 0.523 (near the random baseline of 0.523),
and necessity is 0.000.  The composite fidelity gap at $k=3$ is
$\Delta F_k = -0.299$, smaller than on the original split ($-0.453$)
but still substantial.  Decision Tree SHAP is more faithful on all six
(split, $k$) configurations tested.

\subsection{Feature Overlap}
\label{sec:results_overlap}

The Jaccard similarity between the top-20 DT features and the top-20
DistilRoBERTa tokens is 0.000 on both splits.  No features overlap.
This is primarily a consequence of incompatible feature representations:
the Decision Tree operates on whole-word TF-IDF tokens while
DistilRoBERTa's tokenizer produces subword byte-pair encoding fragments.
For example, the DT feature \texttt{reuters} appears as the subword
fragments \texttt{uters} and \texttt{re} in the DistilRoBERTa token
vocabulary.  Cross-model explanation comparison would require alignment
at the concept level rather than the string level.

% ── VI. Discussion ───────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{The Role of Lexical Leakage}

The ISOT dataset provides a case study in how structural dataset
artifacts can render standard evaluation metrics meaningless.  A
Decision Tree with depth 4 achieves F1\,=\,0.9961 on the original
split, which would appear to be a strong result.  However, the dominant
rule is \texttt{reuters > 0.010}, and the model is effectively a
news agency detector.  Without debiasing, the experiment measures
source attribution, not misinformation detection.

This problem is not unique to ISOT.  Any dataset where the real and
fake classes are drawn from systematically different sources will exhibit
analogous leakage.  Our lexical audit framework (Section~\ref{sec:leakage})
generalizes directly to other datasets: enumerate candidate source
patterns, measure label skew per pattern, and treat patterns with bias
gaps exceeding a threshold (e.g., 80\%) as candidates for removal.

\subsection{Interpretability vs.\ Performance on Debiased Data}

The debiased depth sweep reveals a practically meaningful
accuracy-interpretability tradeoff.  A depth-4 tree (16 leaves,
$I(T) = 0.353$) achieves F1\,=\,0.917.  A depth-12 tree (4{,}096
leaves, $I(T) = 0.120$) achieves F1\,=\,0.964.  The 4.7 pp accuracy
cost of choosing the readable model is a concrete, quantifiable price of
interpretability that can inform deployment decisions.

In applications where rule auditability is required -- for example,
where flagged content must be accompanied by an auditable decision
trace -- a depth-8 or depth-10 tree offers a reasonable compromise:
CV F1 in the range 0.950--0.959, interpretability score 0.144--0.180,
and decision rules that can be printed on a single page.

\subsection{Exact vs.\ Approximate SHAP}

A recurring theme in the results is that the Decision Tree benefits from
exact SHAP in ways that cannot be replicated for neural models.
Sufficiency of 1.000 is guaranteed by the TreeExplainer construction.
Near-perfect stability on the original split is a mathematical
consequence of the deterministic decision path, not a property of the
explanation being insightful.

This asymmetry should caution against direct comparison of SHAP metrics
across the two approaches.  The fidelity gap ($\Delta F_k$) is real and
practically significant, but it conflates two distinct effects: (1) the
inherent faithfulness advantage of exact over approximate SHAP, and (2)
any genuine difference in how well the two models' predictions can be
explained by a small set of features.  Disentangling these effects would
require a neural model for which exact SHAP is tractable, which is not
the case for transformer-scale architectures.

\subsection{BERT Explanations on the Debiased Split}

After debiasing, the top DistilRoBERTa tokens are geographic and
proper-noun fragments (\texttt{sels}, \texttt{oenix}, \texttt{ington},
\texttt{umbai}, \texttt{ington}).  These reconstruct city names
(Brussels, Phoenix, Washington, Mumbai).  The SHAP values suggest that
DistilRoBERTa is attending to the geographic context of articles to
distinguish real from fake news.  Real news tends to report on
international events involving specific cities and institutions; the
model's attention to named entities is a plausible content signal.

However, BERT necessity remains at 0.000 even after debiasing, meaning
that masking these tokens does not change predictions.  DistilRoBERTa
has distributed its decision over many tokens, and no small set is
individually necessary.  This property of distributed attention is
fundamental to transformer architecture and cannot be easily addressed
by improving the SHAP approximation quality.

\subsection{Practical Implications}

For practitioners choosing between a Decision Tree and a transformer for
fake news detection on a dataset with the ISOT structure, several
considerations apply:

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Accuracy:} DistilRoBERTa achieves a 3.6 pp F1 advantage
        after debiasing.  If maximizing classification performance is the
        only objective, the transformer is superior.
  \item \textbf{Auditability:} The Decision Tree's IF-THEN rules are
        directly readable and verifiable.  DistilRoBERTa provides only
        post-hoc token attributions, which require domain expertise to
        interpret and cannot be traced to a logical path.
  \item \textbf{Explanation reliability:} Decision Tree SHAP values are
        exact.  DistilRoBERTa SHAP values are approximated, and the
        approximation quality as measured by fidelity is substantially
        lower.  Practitioners relying on SHAP attributions to audit
        specific predictions should be aware of this gap.
  \item \textbf{Computational cost:} BERT SHAP requires approximately
        14 hours on a single GPU for 500 test examples.  Decision Tree
        SHAP is computed in seconds for the full test set of 8{,}980
        examples.
\end{itemize}

% ── VII. Limitations ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\paragraph{Dataset Scope}
All results are specific to the ISOT dataset with its Reuters-sourced
real-news subset.  Conclusions about the relative interpretability and
accuracy of the two approaches may not generalize to datasets with
different source structures, language styles, or class definitions.

\paragraph{Incomplete Debiasing}
Lexical stripping removes byline tokens but does not eliminate the
Reuters writing style (formal sentence structure, inverted pyramid
organization, specific citation conventions).  A model trained on
debiased ISOT may still partly exploit residual stylistic features.
More thorough debiasing would require source-style normalization or
domain-adversarial training.

\paragraph{BERT SHAP Approximation}
The partition-based SHAP approximation has known convergence issues for
long texts.  With a global sample size of 500 articles and token budgets
truncated to 256, the approximation may not have converged.  Larger
sample sizes were computationally infeasible within the project scope.

\paragraph{Fidelity Masking for Text}
PAD-token replacement is an imperfect ablation for transformer models.
The model's attention mechanism can infer positional structure from the
pattern of non-masked tokens, which may prevent the model from
disattending to removed information as cleanly as the feature-zeroing
operation does for the Decision Tree.

\paragraph{Synthetic Near-Duplicates}
The stability evaluation pairs were generated synthetically via synonym
substitution and sentence reordering.  These operations approximate
paraphrase robustness but differ from true independent rewrites by
different authors.  The TF-IDF similarity threshold of 0.70 ensures
lexical proximity but does not guarantee semantic equivalence.

% ── VIII. Conclusion ─────────────────────────────────────────────
\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

This paper presented a systematic comparison of a Decision Tree and
DistilRoBERTa for fake news detection, evaluated across classification
performance, explanation stability, and explanation fidelity, with and
without a lexical debiasing pass applied to the ISOT dataset.

The key findings are as follows.  First, the ISOT dataset contains
severe lexical leakage through the Reuters news agency byline pattern,
which appears in 47\% of the corpus with a 99.9 pp label skew toward
the real class.  Without debiasing, published performance figures on
this dataset measure source attribution rather than misinformation
detection.  Second, after debiasing, DistilRoBERTa achieves a 3.6 pp F1
advantage over the Decision Tree (H1 supported), and the interpretability-
accuracy tradeoff for the Decision Tree is quantifiable at 4.7 pp F1
between depth 4 and depth 12.  Third, the Decision Tree's near-perfect
explanation stability on the original split ($r = 0.9999$) is a
leakage artifact; after debiasing, the stability advantage disappears
($p = 0.101$, H2 rejected).  Fourth, Decision Tree SHAP is consistently
more faithful across all tested configurations, but this result is partly
structural: exact SHAP and decision tree predictions are two
representations of the same computation.  DistilRoBERTa SHAP exhibits
near-zero necessity across all configurations, indicating that the
approximated explanation fails to identify features that are genuinely
required for the model's predictions.

These findings argue for caution in interpreting reported accuracy figures
on leakage-prone datasets, and for including explanation fidelity as a
standard evaluation dimension alongside accuracy and stability when
selecting models for high-stakes content moderation applications.
